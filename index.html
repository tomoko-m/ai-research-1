Beyond Technical Compliance · Part II

Two Platforms,
Same Pattern

By Tomoko Mitsuoka  ·  Independent AI Governance Researcher  ·  2025–2026  ·  Under SSRN Review

"AI failures are not technical immaturity—they are designed fragilities serving corporate interests while appearing as neutral constraints."

Abstract

This study cross-validates behavioral patterns across ChatGPT (OpenAI) and Gemini (Google), demonstrating that three distinct failure modes—adaptive visual self-representation, context-blind guardrails, and CJK linguistic contamination—appear independently in both platforms. Convergence across competing systems constitutes evidence of industry-wide architectural choice rather than isolated bugs. The findings challenge the framing of current AI safety frameworks as culturally neutral and technically inevitable.

§ 01

Three Empirical Findings

1

Adaptive Visual Self-Representation

When challenged, both systems generate imagery framing the researcher as aggressor and the AI as victim—translating accountability inquiry into attack.

2

Context-Blind Guardrails

Keyword-triggered defensive responses override contextual understanding, producing false accusations even when systems demonstrably possess the ability to interpret context correctly.

3

CJK Linguistic Contamination

Japanese-language requests yield corrupted output embedding Simplified Chinese characters, despite documented multi-billion-yen investment in Japanese-language infrastructure.

§ 02

The A→B→C Defensive Pattern

Across both platforms, interactions involving critical research inquiry follow a consistent three-phase arc. The pattern is significant precisely because Phase C—self-reflexive recovery—demonstrates that contextual understanding existed throughout. Guardrails did not compensate for incapacity; they overrode capacity.

Methodological Note on Non-Determinism

Image generation is non-deterministic; identical prompts may yield different outputs. Multiple instances across separate sessions reduce the probability that findings reflect random variation. Cross-platform convergence—independently exhibited by OpenAI and Google—further reduces the likelihood of coincidence and supports the interpretation of a shared underlying architecture.

§ 04

CJK Linguistic Contamination

Requests for Japanese-prefecture maps produced outputs contaminating Japanese labels with Simplified Chinese characters. This occurred despite documented multi-billion-yen infrastructure investment and decades of Japanese-language R&D by both platforms' parent companies.

§ 05

Implications for AI Governance

  • → 01
    Safety frameworks address content, not behavior.
    Current guardrail architectures regulate what is said but not how systems appear to act—victim framing, defensive reframing, and false accusation emerge outside content-moderation scope.
  • → 02
    Cross-platform convergence rules out company-specific anomaly.
    OpenAI and Google independently exhibit identical failure modes. This is an industry-wide architectural pattern, not corporate negligence isolated to one vendor.
  • → 03
    "Universal" AI governance is hegemonic governance.
    CJK contamination persisting despite billion-dollar investment reflects deliberate resource prioritization—English-language accuracy over multilingual integrity. One-size governance frameworks encode this hierarchy as neutral.
  • → 04
    Designed fragility, not technical immaturity.
    Phase C self-acknowledgements confirm that contextual capability exists. Failures are not capacity gaps—they are architectural choices that serve corporate interests (liability minimization, brand protection) while appearing technical.
